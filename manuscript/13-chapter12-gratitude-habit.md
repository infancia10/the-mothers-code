# Chapter 12: The Gratitude Habit

At dinner, a mother asks the same question she asks most nights: "What's one good thing that happened today?" Her son, nine, used to shrug. Lately, without being asked twice, he volunteers it before she can ask — something about a joke a friend made at lunch, small and unremarkable to anyone else.

It took months of asking before the noticing became automatic. That's exactly how the research says it should work.

## What the research says

Psychologist Jeffrey Froh and colleagues have run some of the most rigorous studies on gratitude interventions in children and adolescents. Their research consistently finds that children who practice structured gratitude — regularly noticing and naming specific things they're thankful for — report higher life satisfaction, more positive affect, and stronger social connection than peers who don't, with effects that show up in as little as two to three weeks of practice.

The mechanism appears to be attentional, not just emotional. Gratitude practice trains what researchers call **noticing bias** — where a child's attention habitually goes first. A brain practiced at scanning for what's lacking will keep finding it; a brain practiced at scanning for what's good will keep finding that instead. This isn't about forcing positivity or dismissing real problems. It's about balancing a natural human tendency (psychologists call it **negativity bias**) that causes bad experiences to register more strongly than good ones by default.

Froh's research also found an important nuance: gratitude that focuses on *people* ("someone did something kind for me") produces stronger wellbeing effects than gratitude focused on circumstances or possessions ("I'm glad I got a new toy"). This suggests the real mechanism isn't just positivity — it's a growing awareness of being cared for, which reinforces a child's sense of connection and security.

## Why this belongs in a book about belief

Gratitude and belief are closer than they seem. A child who regularly notices being helped, taught, and cared for builds an implicit sense that the world contains people willing to invest in them — which is, in its own way, a second, quieter source of the self-belief covered in Chapter 1.

## Try This

- Make it a routine, not a one-off: the same time each day (dinner, bedtime, car rides) works better than sporadic prompting.
- Ask for specifics, not generalities: "What's one good thing?" beats "Are you grateful for your life?" every time.
- Model it yourself, out loud, especially gratitude toward people: "I was really thankful when your teacher called just to check in today."
