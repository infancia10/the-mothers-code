# Chapter 15: Waiting Well

A four-year-old stares down a single marshmallow on a plate, hands tucked under her legs, negotiating with herself out loud: "If I wait, I get two." She lasts almost the full time before giving in. Her mother, watching from the doorway, isn't tracking whether she succeeds. She's noticing that her daughter is developing a strategy at all.

That instinct to strategize, it turns out, may matter more than the willpower itself.

## What the research says

Walter Mischel's famous **marshmallow test**, first run at Stanford in the late 1960s, is often remembered as a study of raw willpower: children who could resist eating one marshmallow for fifteen minutes, in exchange for two later, tended to show better academic and life outcomes years afterward. But Mischel's later, more detailed research revealed something more useful than a simple "some kids have willpower and some don't."

What actually distinguished the children who waited successfully wasn't innate self-control — it was **strategy**. Children who succeeded tended to spontaneously use distraction techniques: looking away, singing, covering their eyes, reframing the marshmallow as "just a cloud" rather than food. Mischel called this **strategic allocation of attention**, and crucially, his follow-up research found these strategies could be taught. Children given simple reframing techniques dramatically improved their ability to wait, regardless of their starting point.

This reframes delayed gratification from a fixed trait to a learnable skill — closely related to the growth mindset research in Chapter 2. A child isn't simply "good" or "bad" at waiting. They either have, or don't yet have, a toolkit for managing the discomfort of wanting something now.

Later replications of the marshmallow test, notably a 2018 study led by Tyler Watts, found the original effect was smaller once researchers controlled for family income and background — an important caveat. Waiting ability is shaped by environment as much as skill; a child whose experience has taught them that promises often don't materialize has a rational reason to take the one marshmallow now.

## Try This

- Teach concrete waiting strategies directly: "Let's think of something else while we wait" or "Let's turn it into a game" — don't assume the skill is innate.
- Keep your promises about delayed rewards scrupulously. Waiting only makes sense if "later" reliably arrives.
- Praise the strategy, not just the outcome: "You found a good way to make the wait easier" teaches something more transferable than "good job waiting."
