# Chapter 16: The Question Behind the Question

"Why is the sky blue?" turns into "But why does light do that?" which turns into "But why?" four more times, somewhere on a walk to nowhere in particular. It would be easy, on the fifth why, to say "just because." His mother doesn't. She says, "That's a really good question — I'm actually not sure. Should we find out together?"

She doesn't have the answer. She has something more useful: the response that keeps the question alive.

## What the research says

Cognitive scientist Susan Engel, who has spent much of her career studying curiosity in children, has found that curious questioning peaks remarkably early — around ages three to five — and then, for many children, quietly declines through the school years. Her research points to a specific culprit: environments, including well-meaning classrooms and homes, that reward *correct answers* far more consistently than they reward *good questions*.

Engel's classroom observation studies found that teachers asked far more questions than students did, and that opportunities for child-initiated inquiry shrank as children moved through school — even though the children's capacity for curiosity hadn't changed. The pattern suggests curiosity isn't something children simply grow out of. It's something environments can inadvertently train out of them, one "just because" and one hurried dismissal at a time.

This connects to Chapter 7's research on intrinsic motivation: curiosity itself is a form of self-directed engagement, and how adults respond to it either reinforces or extinguishes that internal drive. A child whose questions are consistently met with genuine engagement learns that wondering is worthwhile. A child whose questions are consistently brushed off learns, just as effectively, that it isn't.

Notably, Engel's research found that parents don't need to know the answers for this to work. What matters is the *quality of response* — curiosity met with curiosity — not the accuracy of the information provided.

## Try This

- Resist the urge to shut down a question chain with "just because." Even "I don't know, what do you think?" keeps the exploratory door open.
- Turn unanswerable questions into shared investigations: look it up together, or better, guess together before looking it up.
- Notice and name your child's questions as valuable: "That's such an interesting thing to wonder about" reinforces the questioning itself, not just the eventual answer.
