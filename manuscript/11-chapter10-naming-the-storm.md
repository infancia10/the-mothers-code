# Chapter 10: Naming the Storm

A five-year-old melts down in the cereal aisle because the store is out of the right box. A watching stranger's eyebrows say *that's excessive.* His mother doesn't shush him or bribe him quiet. She crouches down and says, "You're really disappointed. You really wanted that one." His crying doesn't stop immediately — but it changes shape. It becomes shorter, softer, findable.

She didn't fix the problem. She named the feeling. It turns out that's most of the work.

## What the research says

Psychologist John Gottman's research on what he calls **emotion coaching** identifies a specific parenting style that predicts better emotional regulation, fewer behavioral problems, and stronger peer relationships in children over time. Emotion-coaching parents do a specific, learnable thing: they notice a child's emotion before it escalates, validate it without judgment, and help the child put words to what they're feeling — rather than dismissing the emotion ("you're fine") or punishing its expression ("stop crying or we're leaving").

The neuroscience behind this has a catchy shorthand popularized by psychiatrist Dan Siegel: **"name it to tame it."** Brain imaging research suggests that putting a feeling into words activates regulatory regions of the prefrontal cortex and measurably reduces activity in the amygdala, the brain's alarm system. In other words, labeling an emotion isn't just a communication tool — it appears to be a genuine, in-the-moment regulation mechanism, for children and adults alike.

Gottman's research also found that children of emotion-coaching parents showed better physiological self-soothing (measured through heart rate and stress hormones) even years later, suggesting the skill of naming emotions doesn't just calm the moment — it builds a durable capacity for self-regulation.

## The trap of jumping to fix

A common instinct is to move straight to solving: offering a replacement, a distraction, a bribe. This isn't wrong, exactly, but research suggests it skips a necessary step. A feeling that's solved before it's named often resurfaces later, bigger, because the child never learned the vocabulary to manage it themselves.

## Try This

- Before offering a solution, offer a label: "You're frustrated," "You're disappointed," "You're scared." Keep it simple and non-judgmental.
- Resist minimizing language ("it's not a big deal," "you're fine"). Even if the trigger seems small to you, the feeling is real to them.
- Build an emotional vocabulary during calm moments too — naming feelings in books, movies, or your own day — so the words are already available when a storm hits.
