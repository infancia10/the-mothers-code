# Chapter 4: Letting Them Fall

A nine-year-old forgets his homework on the kitchen counter for the third time this month. His mother's hand is already on her car keys — it would take eleven minutes to drive it to school. She stops. She lets him walk into class and explain himself to his teacher.

It is one of the hardest small things she will do all year. It is also one of the most important.

## What the research says

Over the past two decades, a growing body of research has documented the effects of what's often called "overparenting" or "helicopter parenting" — the well-intentioned habit of shielding children from struggle, discomfort, and consequence.

A widely cited 2013 study by Holly Schiffrin and colleagues, published in the *Journal of Child and Family Studies*, found that college students with highly controlling, over-involved parents reported significantly higher rates of anxiety and depression, and lower satisfaction with basic psychological needs like autonomy and competence. The children weren't lacking love. They were lacking *practice* — the accumulated, ordinary experience of handling a problem themselves and discovering they survived it.

This connects to a concept psychologists call **learned helplessness**, first documented by Martin Seligman. When a person is repeatedly prevented from experiencing the natural consequences of their actions — good or bad — they don't just fail to develop coping skills. They develop the belief that their own actions don't matter, because someone else always intervenes.

Struggle, in the right dose, is not the opposite of love. It's a form of it. A mother who lets her child feel the discomfort of an avoidable mistake is communicating something profound: *I trust you to handle hard things, because I know you're capable of more than you think.*

## The dose matters

This isn't a license for neglect, and the research doesn't support throwing a child into overwhelming, unsupported situations. The principle is *scaffolded* struggle — letting a child face challenges that are hard but survivable, while staying emotionally available if they truly need you. The goal is to be present for the *emotional* fallout of a mistake, without absorbing the *practical* fallout for them.

## Try This

- Before stepping in to fix a problem, ask yourself: is this dangerous, or just uncomfortable? Only intervene for the former.
- Let natural consequences do the teaching where safe: a forgotten lunch means a hungry hour, not a rescue mission. The lesson lands harder than any lecture would.
- When your child faces a hard consequence, resist the urge to soften it with excuses ("the teacher was too strict"). Instead, sit with the discomfort alongside them: "That's a rough one. What do you think you'll do differently next time?"
