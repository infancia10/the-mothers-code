# Chapter 11: The Comparison Trap

"Why can't you focus like your sister?" It slips out in a tired moment, said lightly, almost as a joke. The words land differently than they were meant. Years later, the daughter can still recite the sentence exactly, word for word, the way people remember lyrics.

No parent means to hand a child a permanent measuring stick. It happens in small sentences, said in passing, that the sibling on the receiving end never quite puts down.

## What the research says

Research on what psychologists call **parental differential treatment** — the different ways parents treat siblings within the same family — consistently finds that children are remarkably accurate at perceiving when they're treated differently, even when parents believe they're being even-handed. Studies led by researchers including Alexander Jensen and Shawn Whiteman have found that children who perceive themselves as receiving less warmth or more criticism than a sibling show higher rates of low self-esteem, depressive symptoms, and strained sibling relationships that can persist into adulthood.

The comparison itself doesn't have to be explicit ("your brother never acts this way") to register. Even implicit comparisons — different tones of praise, different latitude for mistakes, different assumptions about who is "the responsible one" or "the difficult one" — get absorbed by children early and can calcify into identity. A child repeatedly cast as "the easy one" or "the hard one" often grows into that label, a phenomenon closely related to the Pygmalion effect covered in Chapter 1: expectations don't just predict behavior, they help produce it.

Interestingly, the research suggests it's not differential treatment alone that causes harm — some differentiation based on genuine age or need differences is normal and expected. What predicts poorer outcomes is differential treatment that feels *unfair* to the child, especially around warmth and affection rather than practical accommodations.

## Try This

- Catch comparison language before it leaves your mouth, even the affectionate-sounding kind ("she's my easy one"). Labels said often enough become identities.
- When siblings are struggling with the same issue, address each individually rather than holding one up as the standard for the other.
- Ask each child, separately and periodically, "Do you ever feel like I treat you and your sibling differently?" Their answer will often surprise you — and it's far better to hear it directly than to have it surface twenty years later.
