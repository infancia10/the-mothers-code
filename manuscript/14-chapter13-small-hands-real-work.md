# Chapter 13: Small Hands, Real Work

A three-year-old insists on "helping" set the table, which in practice means the process takes four times as long and one glass doesn't survive. It would be faster — infinitely faster — for her mother to just do it herself. She lets her keep trying anyway.

Twenty years later, this is one of the more quietly predictive decisions a parent can make.

## What the research says

One of the most cited findings in this space comes from a longitudinal study led by Marty Rossmann, who analyzed data tracking children from age three or four into their mid-twenties. Rossmann found that the strongest predictor of young adults' success — more than IQ scores, more than family circumstances — was whether they had participated in household chores starting as early as ages three and four. Adults who began chores that young showed better relationships, higher academic and career achievement, and greater self-sufficiency than those who started chores as teenagers or not at all.

Researchers believe the mechanism isn't the chore itself, but what the chore represents: early, repeated experience of being a genuinely needed contributor to a shared effort, rather than a passive recipient of care. This builds what psychologists sometimes call **contributive competence** — the felt sense that one's actions matter to the functioning of a group, distinct from the achievement-based competence covered in Chapter 1.

There's a common pattern researchers have flagged as a modern obstacle: many parents delay involving children in real household work because it's slower and less efficient in the moment, effectively outsourcing "helping" to a later age when it's more convenient. But the Rossmann data suggests this delay may cost more than it saves — the early years of eager, clumsy "helping" are precisely when the identity of being a capable contributor gets built.

## Try This

- Say yes to the slow, messy version of help before age five, even when it costs you time. That inefficient season is doing more than it looks like.
- Frame chores as contribution, not punishment or payment: "Our family needs this done, and you're part of how it gets done" lands differently than a chore chart tied only to allowance.
- Give real responsibilities, not symbolic ones — tasks that would genuinely need doing whether or not your child did them.
