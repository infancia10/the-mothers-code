# Chapter 20: The Long Goodbye

A seventeen-year-old wants to take a job three states away for the summer, alone, with a car her mother doesn't fully trust. Every instinct says no. She says yes anyway, and spends the entire first week refreshing her phone. He calls, unprompted, on the third night, just to talk about nothing in particular.

Letting go, it turns out, is what made the calling-just-because possible.

## What the research says

Psychologist Laurence Steinberg, whose decades of research focus on adolescent development, has found that **autonomy-granting** — parents gradually extending independence and decision-making power as children mature — is one of the strongest predictors of healthy adolescent development, closely tied to the authoritative parenting style discussed in Chapter 19. Crucially, Steinberg's research distinguishes autonomy-granting from simple withdrawal: the parent doesn't step back from the relationship, only from control over decisions that are increasingly the teenager's to make.

This extends the secure base research from Chapter 3 into a new developmental stage. Attachment researchers who study adolescence, including Joseph Allen, have found that teenagers with secure relationships to their parents don't need their parents less — they need a different kind of availability. Allen's research describes this as a shift from a **secure base for exploration** in early childhood to a **secure base for autonomy** in adolescence: the parent's job changes from providing proximity to providing a reliable relationship a teenager can return to, on their own terms, after taking risks the parent didn't get to approve in advance.

Steinberg's research also found that adolescents whose parents were overly controlling during the teen years showed higher rates of anxiety, lower self-reliance, and paradoxically, more risk-taking behavior — often because tightly controlled teenagers have had less practice making and recovering from smaller decisions before the stakes get higher.

## Why this belongs at the end of the code

Every principle in this book, from belief to repair to routine, has been building toward this one. The entire purpose of a secure, believing, emotionally coached, autonomy-supported childhood is to become unnecessary — not unwanted, but unnecessary in the specific sense of no longer being required for your child's basic function in the world. That's not the code breaking down. That's the code working exactly as intended.

## Try This

- Practice granting smaller autonomy well before the big ones arrive — decisions about clothes, schedules, and friendships in childhood build the muscle needed for bigger ones in adolescence.
- When your teenager takes a risk you wouldn't have chosen, resist leading with the lecture. Lead with availability: "I'm glad you called. Tell me everything."
- Remind yourself, especially in the hardest moments of letting go: the goal was never to be needed forever. It was to be trusted enough to let go toward.
