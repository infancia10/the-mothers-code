# Chapter 14: The Screen in the Room

A mother sits on the floor building a block tower with her toddler, phone face-down beside her — until it lights up, and her hand moves toward it almost before she decides to. Her son notices the half-second her eyes leave his. He doesn't have words for what he felt. He just reaches up and puts his hand on her cheek, turning her face back toward his.

He was recreating eye contact that had, for a moment, gone missing.

## What the research says

Researchers Brandon McDaniel and Jenny Radesky coined the term **technoference** to describe everyday interruptions to parent-child interaction caused by mobile devices. Their studies, which observed real parent-child play sessions, found that phone-related interruptions — even brief ones — were associated with more behavioral problems in young children, including more frequent tantrums, whining, and hyperactivity, measured months later.

The proposed mechanism connects directly to territory covered earlier in this book. Chapter 9 discussed Tronick's Still Face research, where infants became distressed by a caregiver's blank, unresponsive expression. Radesky's team has drawn an explicit parallel: a parent absorbed in a phone produces a similar "still face" effect in real time, even while physically present in the room. The child experiences a version of the same disconnection — not from a devastating event, but from an ordinary, unremarkable phone check that happens dozens of times a day.

This doesn't mean phones are forbidden or that every glance at a screen damages a child. The research is specifically about **responsiveness during shared attention** — moments when a child is actively trying to engage a parent. It's less about total screen time and more about whether a parent can be reliably reached when a child reaches first.

## A practical distinction

Researchers in this space are careful to note that not all phone use is equal — checking a message during independent play is different from checking one mid-conversation or mid-request. The goal isn't zero phone use around children; it's protecting the specific windows when a child is actively bidding for connection.

## Try This

- Notice your child's "bids" for attention — a look, a "watch this," a tug on your sleeve — and treat those specific moments as phone-free, even if the rest of the afternoon isn't.
- Narrate phone use instead of hiding it: "I need to answer this one message, then I'm back with you" gives a child a predictable window instead of an invisible rupture.
- Build in visibly phone-free blocks — even fifteen minutes — where the phone is in another room entirely, not just face-down.
