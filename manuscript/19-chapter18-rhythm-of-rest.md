# Chapter 18: The Rhythm of Rest

Every night, the same order: bath, two books, the same song hummed slightly off-key, lights out. A visiting relative once suggested skipping a step, just for one night, to save time. The mother declined. It wasn't stubbornness. She had learned, through many worse nights, that the predictability was doing something the content of the routine wasn't.

The songs and the books could probably be swapped. The rhythm could not.

## What the research says

Sleep researcher Avi Sadeh and colleagues have studied the link between household routines, sleep, and child wellbeing extensively, finding that consistent bedtime routines are associated with better sleep onset, fewer night wakings, and improved daytime emotional regulation in children — effects observable in children as young as infancy. Poor or inconsistent sleep, in turn, is strongly linked to worse emotional regulation, more behavioral problems, and impaired attention, creating a cycle that can be hard to interrupt without addressing the routine itself.

But the research on family routines extends well beyond sleep. A substantial body of work, including reviews by researcher Barbara Fiese, has found that predictable family routines — regular mealtimes, consistent bedtime rituals, stable weekly patterns — are associated with stronger child wellbeing, better academic performance, and even more resilient family relationships during times of stress. Fiese's research suggests routines function as a kind of scaffolding: they reduce the number of decisions and negotiations a child has to navigate in a day, freeing up emotional and cognitive resources for everything else.

This is a different kind of "safety" than the relational safety discussed in Chapter 3, but a related one. Predictability itself appears to communicate security to a developing nervous system — a child who knows roughly what comes next spends less energy bracing for uncertainty.

## The nuance

Researchers are careful to distinguish rigid control from healthy routine. The protective effect comes from predictability and warmth, not from strict enforcement. A routine held too tightly, enforced through anxiety or punishment, loses the very security it's meant to build.

## Try This

- Protect a simple, repeatable bedtime sequence, even a short one — the specific activities matter less than the reliability of the order.
- Extend the same principle to daytime: consistent mealtimes and a predictable weekly rhythm reduce friction more than most parents expect.
- When routines break (travel, illness, chaos), rebuild them calmly rather than treating the disruption as a failure. Consistency over time matters more than any single night.
