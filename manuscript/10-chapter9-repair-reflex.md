# Chapter 9: The Repair Reflex

A mother snaps at her toddler over something small — spilled juice, a missed nap, the fortieth interruption of a long day. The child's face crumples, not from the volume, but from the sudden absence of the warm face she relies on. Ninety seconds later, the mother kneels down, meets his eyes, and says, "I got loud, and that wasn't about you. I'm sorry. Can we try again?" His shoulders drop. He climbs into her lap.

Nothing was undone. But something was repaired.

## What the research says

In the early 1970s, developmental psychologist Edward Tronick ran what became known as the **Still Face Experiment**: a mother would play warmly with her infant, then suddenly hold a blank, unresponsive expression for a short period before resuming normal interaction. Infants reliably became distressed during the still face — reaching, vocalizing, trying everything to restore connection. But the finding that mattered most wasn't the distress. It was what happened after: once the mother resumed warm engagement, infants who experienced consistent repair recovered quickly and returned to secure, exploratory play.

Tronick's broader research suggests something that should be a relief to every mother who has ever lost her patience: securely attached relationships are not defined by an absence of rupture. In fact, his estimates suggest that even in healthy relationships, parents and infants are "in sync" only a fraction of the time — misattunement is the norm, not the exception. What predicts security isn't perfect harmony. It's the reliability of repair afterward.

This is sometimes called the **rupture-repair cycle**, and it may be one of the most important, least talked-about mechanisms in child development. A child who experiences repeated repair learns something no lecture could teach: relationships can survive conflict. Disconnection is not permanent. People come back.

## Why this matters more than the rupture itself

Many mothers carry quiet guilt over the moments they weren't patient, present, or warm. This research reframes that guilt: the goal was never zero ruptures. It's building the reflex to repair — quickly, specifically, without over-explaining or over-apologizing in a way that centers your own guilt instead of the child's experience.

## Try This

- After any sharp moment — raised voice, distracted dismissal, short temper — return within minutes, not hours. Get on their eye level and name what happened simply: "I got frustrated. That wasn't fair to you."
- Avoid folding the repair into a lecture about their behavior. Repair the relationship first; address the behavior separately, later, when both of you are calm.
- Let your child see you repair with others too — apologizing to your partner, a sibling, a friend. Repair modeled in front of them teaches the skill as much as repair directed at them.
