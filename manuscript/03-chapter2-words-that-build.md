# Chapter 2: The Words That Build (or Break) a Mind

Two girls bring home the same math test score: an 88. One mother says, "You're so smart!" The other says, "You really worked hard on those word problems — I could tell."

Six years later, faced with a genuinely difficult class for the first time in her life, one of these girls quietly drops it rather than risk a bad grade. The other struggles publicly, asks for help, and finishes the semester with a B-minus she's proud of.

The difference didn't come from talent. It came from four words, repeated across a thousand small moments.

## What the research says

This is the territory Stanford psychologist Carol Dweck spent decades mapping, and it remains one of the most replicated findings in developmental psychology. Dweck's research distinguishes between two mindsets:

- A **fixed mindset**, where a child believes intelligence and ability are static traits — you either have them or you don't.
- A **growth mindset**, where a child believes ability is built through effort, strategy, and persistence.

In Dweck's studies, children praised for being *smart* after a task performed measurably worse on subsequent challenges than children praised for their *effort or process.* Why? Praise for an innate trait ("smart") makes failure feel like evidence that the trait was never real. Praise for effort makes failure just feel like information — a sign to try a different strategy, not a verdict on identity.

The mechanism is subtle but powerful: the words a mother uses in the ordinary, unremarkable moments — homework, sports, chores — become the internal script a child replays alone, years later, in a job interview or a hard conversation or a business idea that isn't working yet.

## It's not just praise — it's also comfort

The same principle applies in reverse, during failure. "You're just not a math person" is a fixed-mindset sentence disguised as sympathy. It closes a door permanently. "That one didn't click yet — let's find the piece that's confusing you" keeps the door open. Children are remarkably attuned to which sentence they're hearing, even when the tone is equally warm.

## Try This

- Swap trait-praise for process-praise: not "you're brilliant," but "I noticed you didn't give up when the first approach failed."
- Ban the word "yet" from nowhere — add it everywhere. *"I can't do this"* becomes *"I can't do this yet."* It sounds small. Dweck's research shows it measurably changes persistence.
- When your child fails at something, resist comforting with a fixed-trait excuse ("you're just not artistic"). Instead, get curious together: "What do you think would help next time?"
